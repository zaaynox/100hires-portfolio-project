# AI-Powered SEO Content Production Playbook
## A Practical SOP for B2B SaaS Companies in 2026

**Author:** Parishrut Singh Bisht  
**Research completed:** April 2026  
**Topic:** AI-Powered SEO Content Production

---

## Executive Summary

Search is no longer a single channel. It is an ecosystem of surfaces — Google blue links, AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Claude — each pulling from overlapping but distinct pools of content. A B2B SaaS company that only optimizes for traditional Google rankings is leaving significant visibility on the table. A company that chases AI citations without genuine topical authority will see short-term gains followed by a collapse.

This playbook is built on research across 10 practitioners who are actively running campaigns, testing algorithms, and publishing original data. It is not a summary of their views. It is a synthesis with explicit positions taken where experts disagree, clear reasoning for what was rejected, and one original idea that does not appear anywhere in the source material.

The core argument is this: in 2026, the winning content strategy for B2B SaaS combines human-directed AI execution with original insight density, structured for both traditional search and AI retrieval, anchored by real brand signals that no amount of technical optimization can manufacture.

---

## The Core Framework

Three principles govern every decision in this playbook.

**Principle 1: Original insight density over volume**

Nick Jordan's content velocity model proved that publishing at scale builds topical authority faster than almost any other method. That was true when volume was expensive and therefore a signal of legitimacy. AI writing tools have made volume cheap, which means volume alone no longer signals quality to Google or to LLMs. The new signal is how many genuinely new ideas, data points, or perspectives exist per piece of content. High velocity with generic content will trigger the same outcome as the helpful content update victims Lily Ray describes. Moderate velocity with high original insight density is the correct calibration for 2026.

**Principle 2: Content infrastructure before brand investment**

Eli Schwartz argues convincingly that brand marketing is the ceiling of LLM visibility. A brand that nobody has heard of will not be recommended regardless of how well its content is optimized. Bernard Huang argues that content targeting the AI reasoning chain is the mechanism for getting into citations at all. Both are right at different stages. The sequence matters: build topical authority and content infrastructure first so the model has something to retrieve, then invest in brand signals to raise the ceiling of how often and how confidently you get recommended. Most B2B SaaS companies have neither, so start with content.

**Principle 3: AI as execution engine, human as strategy layer**

The risk Lily Ray identifies is real and growing. Google is detecting content produced without meaningful human oversight and the algorithmic response is increasingly aggressive. The sustainable model is human-directed AI execution. Humans decide what to write, what angle to take, what original data or insight to include. AI handles the drafting, formatting, and structural optimization. Never let AI decide the editorial direction. Always let it accelerate the execution of a direction you have already chosen.

## Step by Step SOP

### Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1 to 2)

**Step 1: Map the AI reasoning chain for your core topics**

Before writing a single piece of content, understand how AI models actually research your category. Open ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity. Ask each one your most important commercial query, for example "best project management software for remote teams." Expand the reasoning panel where available and note every sub-search the model performs. These sub-searches are your content gaps.

Source: Bernard Huang, BuzzStream Podcast, April 2026 — "You can peel back the layer on how all of these different AI models work by looking at how they're thinking about the prompt that you've given it."

**Step 2: Audit your current entity signals**

Run your brand name through Google, ChatGPT, and Gemini. Note whether you appear, where you appear, how you are described, and whether the description is accurate. This becomes your baseline. Check for the basic trust signals Kyle Roof identifies: physical address on site, a local phone number, current copyright, an about page with real humans, LinkedIn links, and organization schema in your code.

Source: Kyle Roof, The Future Podcast with Chris Brandt — "Do you have an address on your website? Do you have a local phone number that matches the zip code to that address? Do you have a page that talks about the humans that work at your company?"

**Step 3: Cluster your keyword universe**

Export your full keyword list from Ahrefs or Semrush. Use a keyword clustering tool to group them into individual page targets. Do not assume that topically similar keywords belong on the same page. Google's own index determines what belongs together. Two keywords that seem related often need separate pages. Two that seem different sometimes need to be combined.

Source: Nick Jordan, Niche Pursuits Podcast — "You look at two keywords and you're like these two keywords totally belong on the same page and then you google both of those keywords and the results are completely different."

**Step 4: Prioritize by commercial value and competition gap**

From your clustered keyword list, identify topics where your domain has a realistic chance of ranking within 90 days. Kyle Roof's framework applies here: if your site gets 100 clicks per day, target keywords with monthly search volume in that range first. Going after terms with 10x your current traffic before you have earned the authority is a waste of resources.

Source: Kyle Roof, The Future Podcast with Chris Brandt — "If you're at that hundred, you're going after a term that's like 3000 a month, that's going to take some months."

---

### Phase 2: Content Production (Ongoing)

**Step 5: Brief every piece with an original angle mandate**

Before drafting anything, every piece must have a defined original angle. This is non-negotiable. An original angle means one of the following: proprietary data or customer research, a counterintuitive position backed by evidence, a synthesis of two ideas that have not been connected before, or a specific named framework that did not exist before you created it. Generic best-practices content with no original angle should not be commissioned at all.

Source: Lily Ray, The Edward Show — "The more you're just leaning on tools to do the thinking for you, I think the more that that's going to not work over time. You should be able to demonstrate that you have your own original insights."

**Step 6: Structure content for AI extractability**

Kevin Indig's research on 6.8 million subheadings provides the most concrete structural guidance currently available. Keep subheadings between 20 and 39 characters for the highest citation rate. Phrase 25 to 50 percent of subheadings as questions that mirror how users actually prompt AI systems. Avoid subheadings over 60 characters as they correlate with a 6 percentage point drop in citation rates.

Source: Kevin Indig, LinkedIn, April 2026 — analysis of 6.8 million subheadings showing question-format headings match AI fanout queries at 1.5x the rate of declarative headings, with the 20 to 39 character range correlating with the highest citation rate at 32.7%.

**Step 7: Use AI for drafting, not ideation**

Feed your brief including the original angle mandate, target keywords, required subheading structure, and word count target into your AI writing tool. Generate a draft. Have a human editor review for original insight density. If the draft could have been written about any company in your category, it lacks original angle and needs revision before it goes anywhere near publication.

**Step 8: Build internal linking systematically**

Before publishing, identify the 5 to 10 most commercially important pages on your domain. Every new piece of content must include at least one internal link to each of those pages where contextually appropriate. This is not optional. Internal linking is the most underrated and most consistently effective tactic in this entire playbook.

Source: Lily Ray, The Edward Show — "Internal linking is something you can just do forever. Things that help search engines and users simultaneously to navigate your site."

Source: Nick Jordan, Niche Pursuits Podcast — "I think internal links do what people think backlinks do."

**Step 9: Publish at sustainable velocity**

The target is the highest velocity at which original insight density can be maintained. For most B2B SaaS companies with a lean content team this is 8 to 15 pieces per month. Do not publish below this threshold for more than two consecutive months. Topical authority requires consistent signals over time and gaps in publishing send the wrong signal to both Google and LLMs.

---

### Phase 3: Brand Signal Amplification (Months 2 onward)

**Step 10: Run Digital PR targeting already-ranked pages**

Identify the top-ranking pages for your most important commercial queries. Pursue inclusion in those specific pages rather than chasing general press coverage. A mention in a page that ranks number one for your target query is worth more for AI visibility than coverage in a publication that does not rank for anything relevant to your category.

Source: Bernard Huang, BuzzStream Podcast, April 2026 — "You're going to get a lot more bang for your buck because that high ranking website is not only going to be crawled faster, it's going to be seen as more authoritative by the AI models."

**Step 11: Build author entity signals for your key contributors**

Identify 2 to 3 people at your company who will be the public-facing experts. Each needs a LinkedIn profile with consistent positioning, contributions to industry publications, speaker credits where possible, and an author page on your site with a biography and links to their external profiles. These people become associated entities that reinforce your brand's topical authority in both Google's knowledge graph and LLM training data.

Source: Aleyda Solis, LinkedIn — "The stronger and more consistently reinforced your entity is across the web, the more likely AI systems are to identify and represent your brand accurately."

**Step 12: Track prompt-level brand visibility monthly**

Set up a monthly tracking process. Run your 5 most important commercial prompts through ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity. Record whether your brand appears, where it appears in the response, how it is described, and whether the description is accurate. Traffic alone is no longer a sufficient success metric.

Source: Kevin Indig, LinkedIn, April 2026 — "Being cited generally means an AI is drawing on your content, while being mentioned means it is actually naming you. They are two distinct outcomes."

Source: Aleyda Solis, LinkedIn — "Organic traffic does not show whether a brand is being surfaced, cited, or recommended inside AI-generated answers, especially when no click happens."

## Where Experts Disagree

### Disagreement 1: Is brand marketing or content optimization the primary lever for LLM visibility?

**Eli Schwartz's position:** Brand marketing is the primary lever. Companies with strong brand equity will appear in LLMs with minimal additional optimization effort. AEO checklists, AI-generated content, and Reddit citation campaigns are largely ineffective. The best way to improve LLM visibility is through brand marketing, not technical tactics.

Source: Eli Schwartz, LinkedIn — "The best way to improve LLM visibility is through brand marketing, NOT the AEO checklist sold by AEO agencies."

**Bernard Huang's position:** Content targeting the AI reasoning chain is the primary lever. By mapping how AI models research a topic and creating content that appears at each step of that process, brands can systematically increase their citation surface area regardless of how well-known they are.

Source: Bernard Huang, BuzzStream Podcast, April 2026 — "You just create relevant high quality content that targets the AI reasoning chain."

**My position:** Both are right but Schwartz is describing the ceiling and Huang is describing the floor. A brand with no content infrastructure but strong brand equity will be mentioned occasionally based on training data. A brand with strong content infrastructure but weak brand equity will appear in retrieval-augmented responses but rarely in unprompted recommendations. For most B2B SaaS companies that are not yet household names in their category, Huang's framework is more immediately actionable. Schwartz's framework becomes relevant once the content foundation exists. Sequence matters: content first, then brand amplification.

---

### Disagreement 2: Does publishing at high velocity still build authority in the AI content era?

**Nick Jordan's position:** Content velocity is the primary driver of topical authority. The more content you publish about a specific topic, the better all of that content performs. He has taken multiple sites from zero to 100k organic visitors per month using volume as the primary lever, without link building or technical SEO.

Source: Nick Jordan, Niche Pursuits Podcast — "The more content we publish about a specific subtopic the better all of that content does."

**Lily Ray's position:** Volume is now a liability if it comes at the expense of quality. Google's helpful content update was a direct response to the proliferation of scaled low-quality content. Sites that published large volumes of mediocre content lost 85 to 95 percent of their visibility overnight and many have still not recovered.

Source: Lily Ray, The Edward Show — "I am very much of the mindset right now that less is more in SEO."

**My position:** Lily Ray is correct for 2026, but the principle behind Jordan's model is still valid. His velocity approach was built when volume was expensive and therefore a genuine signal of investment and legitimacy. AI writing tools have broken that signal because volume is now cheap. However his underlying principle — that topical depth and comprehensive coverage build authority — remains true. The correct update is to pursue topical depth at a velocity that preserves original insight density, not to abandon velocity altogether.

---

### Disagreement 3: Should you optimize content specifically for AI extractability or focus on serving human readers?

**Kevin Indig's position:** Specific structural optimizations significantly improve AI citation rates. Question-format subheadings, optimal character counts, and content chunked for machine retrieval all produce measurable improvements in how often content gets cited by AI systems.

Source: Kevin Indig, LinkedIn, April 2026 — data from 6.8 million subheadings showing specific structural patterns correlate with higher ChatGPT citation rates.

**Lily Ray's position:** Over-optimizing for AI extractability is risky. When everyone starts applying the same structural patterns, Google identifies it as a tactic and penalizes it. Content that is clearly engineered for machines rather than humans creates the same backlash as every other over-optimization tactic before it.

Source: Lily Ray, The Edward Show — "I'm a little skeptical of that stuff because I think if everyone starts to do it too much, Google's going to say this is skewed way too far from producing good content for humans."

**My position:** Apply Indig's structural guidance as a default template, not an obsession. The subheading length and format recommendations are low-effort, low-risk changes that improve readability for humans and retrievability for machines at the same time. They do not require sacrificing editorial quality. The higher-complexity embedding and cosine similarity optimizations that Lily Ray is skeptical of are a different category entirely and carry genuine over-optimization risk. Use the simple structural guidance. Avoid the deep technical gaming.

## What I Rejected and Why

### Rejection 1: Reddit citation campaigns and self-promotional listicles

Multiple sources in this research reference tactics where brands create Reddit comments or publish articles naming themselves as the best solution in their category in order to influence LLM training data and citations. These tactics do produce short-term results.

I rejected them for a straightforward reason. Lily Ray documents that Claude is already explicitly flagging spammed categories and telling users it is using third-party sources instead of self-promotional ones. The countermeasure is already live. More importantly these tactics create a permanent association risk. Getting caught gaming an LLM creates a negative sentiment signal that is harder to reverse than simply not appearing. The short-term citation gain is not worth the reputational liability when the model learns to flag your brand as a manipulator. The downside is asymmetric and the window is closing fast.

Source: Lily Ray, The Edward Show — "I've literally seen Claude in the last few weeks give me the exact same response — here's some SEO agencies, but I do want to let you know this is a very spammed category."

### Rejection 2: Fully automated AI content publishing pipelines

Several tools and practitioners currently offer fully automated content production where AI generates, optimizes, and publishes content without meaningful human review. The appeal is obvious: near-zero marginal cost per article at high velocity.

I rejected this approach because the core argument of this playbook is that original insight density is the primary differentiator in the AI content era. A fully automated pipeline cannot produce original insight by definition. It can only recombine existing information. Automated pipelines produce content that looks like everything else, which is precisely what gets filtered out by both Google and LLMs that are increasingly trained to surface distinctive sources. Beyond that, Lily Ray is clear that Google can detect content produced without human oversight and the response is getting more aggressive over time, not less.

Source: Lily Ray, The Edward Show — "Just because you can do it in a more sophisticated way doesn't mean it's not against their guidelines."

## My Original Ideas

### The Insight Debt Audit

None of the 10 experts in this research discuss what I would call an insight debt audit, and I think it is one of the highest-leverage activities a B2B SaaS content team can run before beginning any AI content production.

The idea is this. Before producing any new content, systematically audit your existing content library to identify every piece that makes a claim without citing a primary source, every statistic attributed to a secondary aggregator rather than an original study, and every opinion presented as fact. This is your insight debt. Replace each unsourced claim with either a primary source citation or original research. Delete content that cannot be supported.

Why would this work? LLMs do not just retrieve content. They build entity associations based on the quality and provenance of the claims a source makes. A domain that consistently cites primary sources trains the model to treat it as a high-authority reference. A domain full of unsourced claims and recycled statistics trains the model to treat it as a low-authority aggregator. The insight debt audit is a way to systematically upgrade your domain's credibility signal for both Google and LLMs at the same time.

This idea also bridges the tension between Eli Schwartz's brand-first argument and Bernard Huang's content-infrastructure argument. Original well-sourced content creates exactly the kind of brand associations that Schwartz says LLMs respond to, while also building the topical authority that Huang says enables citation retrieval. It is the same activity serving both goals simultaneously.

## Weaknesses of This Playbook

**The AI retrieval layer may not stay stable.** This entire playbook assumes that optimizing for the grounding layer — the real-time web search AI models use to validate their answers — is a durable strategy. Bernard Huang himself acknowledges that model updates could change the rules entirely. A shift away from retrieval-augmented generation toward models relying purely on training data would make content-based optimization far less effective overnight.

**Original insight density is not yet measurable.** The playbook argues that original insight density is the primary quality signal in the AI content era. This is a logical argument, not a proven one. There is no published research that directly measures how original insight density correlates with AI citation rates. It is plausible and directionally supported by what Lily Ray and Eli Schwartz say about authentic content performing better, but it is not empirically validated yet.

**The playbook assumes internal expertise exists.** Producing content with genuine original angles requires people inside the company who can generate real perspectives worth sharing. Many B2B SaaS companies, particularly early-stage ones, do not have that bandwidth. This playbook names what quality requires but does not solve the talent problem behind it.

**Video and podcast content is not covered.** Eli Schwartz specifically calls out YouTube as an underrated lever for Google AI visibility. That is a meaningful gap. A more complete version of this playbook would address how to build a video and audio presence that feeds into AI citation signals, not just written content.

## Who I Would Not Recommend Following and Why

### Nick Jordan

I want to be precise here because Nick Jordan is clearly intelligent and his results were real. The content velocity model worked. The problem is that it worked in a specific window of time under specific conditions that no longer exist, and his publicly available framework has not been updated to account for what has changed.

His core argument was built on case studies from a period when AI writing tools did not exist and when volume was genuinely expensive to produce. His most referenced achievement, taking a client from zero to 1.5 million organics per month, was executed before the helpful content update, before AI content proliferation, and before Google's current posture toward scaled content.

The practitioners in this research who are actively analyzing 2025 and 2026 data, specifically Lily Ray and Eli Schwartz, are seeing the opposite pattern. Sites that built authority through volume are getting demoted. Less is more. Google is rewarding fewer better pieces over more average ones.

Following Nick Jordan's framework in 2026 without significant modification — specifically without adding the original insight density requirement and without reducing volume targets to a level where quality can actually be maintained — is likely to produce the same outcome as the helpful content update casualties Lily Ray describes in detail. The strategy is not wrong in principle. It is wrong in its current form for the current environment. And Jordan has not publicly addressed this gap.

This is not a criticism of his character or his intelligence. It is a judgment that his publicly available framework is calibrated for a market that no longer exists.
