## Post 1
**Topic:** Chrome visit data as a popularity signal backed by Google antitrust trial evidence

Many people have noticed Google rankings rising after receiving lots of visits from other sources. In the Google antitrust trial, evidence emerged that popularity is based on both links and Chrome visit data, and this data is separate from Google search results. The Google API leak further revealed two data endpoints: uniqueChromeViews which counts unique visits in Chrome, and chromeInTotal which measures site-level Chrome views. This could explain why sites appear more popular to Google when users visit through multiple channels including social media, advertising, and newsletter traffic. Google may simply be looking at where people spend their time online and using it as a popularity signal.

## Post 2
**Topic:** Why time on site is one of the most underrated metrics for SEO despite not being a direct ranking factor

Time on site may sound counterintuitive as an SEO metric but evidence from Google's antitrust trial shows Google measures very similar metrics such as return to SERP to use in its ranking algorithms. Multiple Google patents describe using visit length sliced by query, location, and language to distinguish between good clicks and bad clicks, and the Google API leak supports this. Many correlation studies have found a positive relationship between time on site and Google rankings. While it is not a direct ranking factor, time on site is something marketers can influence and it can lead to better user experiences. Marketing teams rarely discuss improving engagement, leaving it to product and engineering teams, but marketers can and should do better here.

## Post 3
**Topic:** New research confirms Google rankings are highly correlated with ChatGPT citations and fan-out queries create a second opportunity

Research from AirOps shows that 55.8% of all pages cited by ChatGPT ranked in the top 20 for at least one query. Less obviously, fan-out queries create a second citation opportunity, with 32.9% of cited pages that appeared in any top 20 SERP discovered only through fan-out. Most marketing and content teams are focused on ranking for head keywords and only starting to consider fan-out queries. The challenge is that fan-out queries can grow exponentially, requiring collation across lots of prompts to find the most high-value content gaps in an AI strategy.

## Post 4
**Topic:** Study of 15K URLs shows major content rewrites vastly outperform minor updates in preventing ranking decay

A study of 15,000 URLs shows that articles receiving major rewrites of 30 to 100% of content vastly outperform minor and moderate updates. Even minor content updates helped prevent decay, but content with no update performed the worst. Interestingly even minor rewrites defined as 0 to 30% of content still saw decay, although not as much. This may relate to the lastSignificantUpdate signal found in the Google API leak, described as a timestamp indicating the last time a document underwent a substantial content change, distinguishing it from minor cosmetic edits. Content updates work to prevent ranking decay, but significant content updates may work much better.

## Post 5
**Topic:** Google's ABC ranking signals straight from antitrust trial testimony explain how topicality is determined

Google's ABC signals are the three fundamental signals of ranking according to antitrust trial testimony from Google Engineer HJ Kim. A stands for Anchors, meaning a source page pointing to a target page via links. B stands for Body, meaning terms in the document. C stands for Clicks, meaning user interaction signals such as how long a user stayed at a particular linked page before bouncing back to the SERP. ABC signals help Google determine topicality, asking whether a document is relevant, engaging, and useful. Other signals discussed include quality scores, eDeepRank, and PageRank. One notable line from the interview states that nowadays people still complain about quality and AI makes it worse.
