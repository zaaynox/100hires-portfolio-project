## Post 1
**Topic:** The difference between AI citations and brand mentions and why optimizing for links alone is not enough

We often assume that getting a link in an AI overview means people are seeing our brand. Around 62% of brand citations fly under the radar based on Semrush data analysis. The domain gets a source link but the brand name is left out of the actual answer text. Only about 13% of appearances converted into both a citation and a mention. Being cited generally means an AI is drawing on your content, while being mentioned means it is actually naming you. They are two distinct outcomes. Optimizing for a link might not automatically get your name in front of users, so it is worth considering both angles when planning content.

## Post 2
**Topic:** Analysis of 6.8 million subheadings reveals what heading structure actually drives ChatGPT citation rates

An analysis of 6.8 million subheadings reveals a measurable correlation between specific heading structures and ChatGPT citation rates. Subheading formatting appears to act as a significant sorting signal for AI retrieval systems. The research examined H2 to H4 headings across 815,000 query and page pairs to identify patterns associated with higher AI visibility. Question style headings appear to align more naturally within the embedding space, matching fanout queries at 1.5 times the rate of declarative headings. The 20 to 39 character range correlates with the highest citation rate at 32.7%. Headings over 60 characters correspond with a 6 percentage point drop in citation rates, while extremely short headings under 20 characters show poor matching. Testing a structure where a quarter to half of your subheadings are phrased as user questions is a data-backed starting point. Structuring H2s to mirror natural user prompts rather than traditional blog titles may significantly improve the probability of being cited.

## Post 3
**Topic:** Usability study shows AI Mode nearly eliminates the comparison shopping phase for major purchases

What happens to the comparison shopping phase when an AI model summarizes the options for you? It almost entirely disappears. A usability study of 185 major-purchase tasks compared how users build shortlists in traditional search versus AI Mode. In classic search, 56% of participants built their own shortlist from scratch using multiple sources. In AI Mode, 74% of final shortlists came directly from the AI output with no external check. Across all 149 AI Mode tasks, 64% of users clicked nothing at all. Instead of clicking through to review sites or retailer pages to evaluate options, users simply read the AI text and declared their finalists. The comparison process did not just shrink. For most participants it did not happen at all. When the search engine acts as a recommendation environment, buyers delegate the cognitive effort of comparison directly to the AI.
